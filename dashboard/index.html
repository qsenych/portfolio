Overview

For UBC ThunderBikes’ 2024 race bike, I was tasked with designing and building a new dashboard. The goal was to give the rider real-time diagnostic data and essential race information in a simple, reliable format, all while withstanding the demands of competition.

Part Selection

The long-term plan was to create a custom PCB with board-mounted switches to simplify assembly. Since our team had extensive experience with STM32 microcontrollers, and I was already comfortable with their ecosystem, I decided to stick with STM32 for faster development and easier integration.

To keep things manageable for my first dashboard project, I opted for a small (3.5", 320×240) parallel-interface display. This allowed me to work with a single frame buffer in memory, simplifying the graphics pipeline. I chose an STM32 with enough RAM to store a full screen in memory and with built-in LTDC (LCD-TFT Controller) support, avoiding the complexity of third-party display drivers.

For the UI, I used the LVGL graphics library, which had great documentation and examples of similar embedded applications.

Prototype creation

Before committing to a PCB, I wanted to validate the entire stack, LVGL → STM32 LTDC → parallel display, on a breadboard setup. I used an STM32F767ZI Nucleo eval board and a breakout module for the display’s ribbon cable so I could hand-solder all the connections myself.

Challenges

The first prototype required soldering ~30 fine-pitch wires from the display to the microcontroller. This was time-consuming and introduced a lot of potential for signal noise and miswiring, but it also gave me valuable hands-on experience with hardware bring-up and troubleshooting display timing issues.
